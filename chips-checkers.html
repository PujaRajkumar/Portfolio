01 — The Brief

A café where games and food collide.

Design a complete brand identity for Chips & Checkers — a fictional board game café in Toronto serving pizza, nachos, coffee, and cocktails. The brand needed to feel warm enough to be a neighbourhood spot, bold enough to stand out on a busy street, and clever enough to reward people who pay attention.

The brief covered everything: naming, logo system, color palette, typography, packaging, and digital applications — all working together as one cohesive brand.

"Most board game cafés go dark and moody. We went bold and confident — because the brand is about joy, not atmosphere."

02 — The Name

Every word earns its place.

The name does double duty. Chips are both poker chips and the British English word for fries. Checkers is both the board game and the act of checking in and paying your bill. That double meaning became the foundation for every visual decision that followed.

A good brand name should be easy to say, impossible to forget, and carry an emotion before you even see the logo. Chips & Checkers tells you exactly what the place is — in two words.

03 — Color System

One signature color. Used ruthlessly.

Black, white, and one pop of Proper Orange (#FF6B00). Not food orange. Not warning orange. A specific, ownable, Hermès-level orange that becomes the brand before you read a single word.

The 60-30-10 rule applied without exception — 60% white, 30% black, 10% Proper Orange. The orange only appears where it needs to hit hardest: the "&" in the logo, the bottom-right square in the icon, the price on a menu item.

04 — Typography

Two fonts. One "&". Infinite personality.

Every brand uses exactly two fonts. A display font for headlines and the logo — this is the personality. And a body font for supporting text — this is the readability. They create contrast. They never compete.

06 — Brand Pattern

The checkerboard is the brand.

A checkerboard pattern. It sounds obvious — but the execution matters. The pattern adapts to its surface: black and white on dark backgrounds, black and orange on orange surfaces. Always used as a band, never full bleed. It wraps the bottom of every application like a signature gesture.

08 — Brand Voice

How the brand talks.

Visual identity is half the brand. The other half is how it sounds. Every word on every surface — from the menu to the inside of a pizza box — follows the same voice principles.

Principle 01
Warm but confident.
Friendly but never overthought. Short sentences. Real words. "Your move, our snacks" not "Welcome to an elevated gaming experience."
Principle 02
Game references always.
Every headline has a game nod. "Your move." "Roll the dice." "Draw four." The brand never forgets what it is.
Principle 03
Never too serious.
Official Rules of Pizza. Drink names like Wild Card Cocktail. The brand earns trust by not taking itself too seriously.
09 — What I Learned

The thinking is the work.

1
Brand identity is mostly decisions, not software.
The name, the colour, the rule about how orange is used — those decisions took longer than building anything in Figma. The software is just the last step. The thinking is the work.
2
The "&" taught me the most.
One character, one font, one colour. It appears in the logo, on the pizza box, in every Instagram post, on the back of the coffee cup. It became the brand's signature without ever trying to. That's what a system does — small decisions compound into something recognisable.
3
Restraint is harder than abundance.
The temptation is always to add more — more colors, more fonts, more elements. The hardest design decision was keeping orange at 10%. Every time it was used less, it hit harder.
4
Every surface is a conversation.
The inside lid of a pizza box nobody expects to have design. That's exactly why it should. The brand that rewards attention is the brand people remember and share.
