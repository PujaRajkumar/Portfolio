01 — User Research

The app that makes coffee feel like work.

I analysed 1-star and 2-star reviews on the App Store and Google Play to identify what was genuinely frustrating real users. Rather than treating the aggregate rating as the truth, I focused on qualitative feedback — the specific words people used when the app failed them.

Five pain points emerged consistently across hundreds of reviews. Critically, they aren't five separate problems — they're one systemic failure: an app built around features, not around the person trying to get a coffee in under 2 minutes.

"Even if you order the same thing at the same time, every day, you need to jump through 10 screens to place the order." — Google Play review

1
No persistent reorder
Daily users rebuild their order from scratch every single morning. No memory of past orders, no one-tap shortcut, no saved customizations.
"Same coffee every day. I have to select everything from scratch. Every. Single. Time."
2
Forced re-login constantly
Sessions expire frequently. Users are kicked out mid-commute and sent verification codes by email — requiring them to switch apps while on a subway platform with poor signal.
"I just want a coffee. Not to check my email for a code while standing on a platform."
3
Apple Wallet & payment failures
Apple Pay fails silently or isn't available. Users are pushed to preload a Tims Card instead. Gift cards can't be added. Balances disappear between sessions.
"Why am I forced to preload money onto a card I don't want just to pay for coffee?"
4
Orders don't sync with stores
Users place mobile orders, arrive at the store, and staff have no record. They rejoin the counter queue anyway — defeating the entire value proposition of the app.
"Ordered ahead. Showed up. They had no idea. Stood in line anyway. What's the point?"
5
Slow load & scan screen glitches
The app takes too long to launch. The scan screen gets stuck loading. For someone opening the app at 8am with one bar of signal, this is a deal-breaker.
"By the time the app loaded I'd already ordered at the counter."
02 — User Persona

Designing for Nadia.

Every design decision in this project was made for one person: Nadia, 28, a registered nurse in Toronto who visits Tim Hortons every morning before her shift. She represents the app's most valuable and most underserved user — the daily commuter who just needs their coffee in under 30 seconds.

04 — Real Problems, Real Fixes

5 moments that broke the experience.

These aren't hypothetical pain points. These are real screenshots from real users — including the app as it exists today. Each one shows a moment where the app failed someone trying to get a coffee. Each one has a direct fix in the redesign.

The problem
1
Promo before Order
2
Order buried in tile
3
10 icons before coffee
4
No reorder anywhere
What's wrong
A Roll Up to Win promo appears before you can order. The Order button is a generic tile — not a shortcut. 10 feature icons (Financial, NHL, Word Challenge) sit between the user and their coffee. There is no reorder anywhere on screen.
The fix
☕
Usual order — front and center
In the redesign, the first thing Nadia sees is her usual order — Medium Dark Roast, no sugar, with price and location already populated. One tap starts the order. The promo is moved below as a subtle card. Feature icons are removed from the home screen entirely.
Design principle
"Show the most likely action first. Everything else is available but never in the way."
9→3
Steps to order reduced from 9 to 3 by surfacing the usual order immediately
The problem
1
Email code required
2
Code also fails
What's wrong
Every sign-in requires a verification code sent by email. Then the code itself fails with "An error occurred logging in." The user is locked out — no fallback, no explanation, no support path.
The fix
🔐
Persistent session + Face ID fallback
The redesign uses persistent session tokens stored securely on device. Nadia is never asked to verify her email. If a session does expire — after weeks of inactivity — Face ID is offered as the one-tap fallback. No email. No code. No failure state.
Design principle
"Every extra authentication step is a moment where the user can fail. Eliminate steps that don't add security."
The problem
1
Vague error message
2
"Yes, delete" — delete WHAT?
What's wrong
"Something went wrong. An error occurred after logging in." Then a single button: "Yes, delete." Delete what? Account? Order? Data? This is one of the most terrifying UX patterns possible — a destructive action with no context.
The fix
💬
Clear error states with safe recovery
Every error in the redesign tells the user exactly what happened and what to do next — in plain language. Destructive actions always require explicit confirmation with a clear explanation. "Delete" is never a primary CTA. Error states include a retry and a support link.
Design principle
"Error messages should tell the user what happened, why, and what to do next. 'Something went wrong' does none of these things."
The problem
1
Can't order — tip bug
2
Apple Pay not default
What's wrong
"Unable to Place Order — Please submit a valid tip amount." The app blocks checkout because of a tip field validation bug. Apple Pay is listed as an option but isn't the default — users must scroll through payment methods to find it.
The fix
💳
Apple Pay as default — one Face ID tap
The redesign makes Apple Pay the default payment method — no scrolling, no selection. The tip field is removed from the critical path entirely. Face ID confirms the entire transaction in one biometric tap. No forms, no validation errors, no blocked checkout.
Design principle
"The checkout flow should have zero optional fields that can block payment. Every form field is a potential failure point."
The problem
1
-400 points mystery
2
Points buried in history tab
What's wrong
The rewards history shows "+35 pts" and immediately "-400 pts" with no explanation. Points disappear with no clarity on why. Balance is buried in a History tab inside Rewards — not visible on the home screen. Users can't trust their points balance.
The fix
⭐
Points on the home screen — updated in real time
In the redesign, Nadia's points balance is shown immediately on the greeting — no navigating to Rewards. After every order, points update instantly with a clear "+24 points" confirmation. Any deduction includes a plain-language explanation. Trust is built through transparency.
Design principle
"Loyalty programmes only work when users trust them. Unexplained point deductions destroy that trust instantly."

Deal Popup — The Non-Blocking Upsell

After reviewing her order, a bottom sheet slides up with 3 relevant morning add-ons. Her order summary is still visible dimmed behind it. One tap adds, one tap dismisses. The primary action is never held hostage.

⏱️
Appears after order review — not before
She's already in buying mode when this appears. Showing it earlier would feel like an interruption. Showing it now feels like a service.
✕
Close button top right — immediately visible
One tap to dismiss. No dark patterns. The close button is the same size as the add buttons — equal affordance for yes and no.
3️⃣
Three options maximum
Decision fatigue is real at 8:43am. More than 3 options and she'll dismiss without reading. Three is the psychological sweet spot for impulse choices.
💬
"Just the coffee" — not "No thanks"
The dismiss copy is warm and specific. It tells her what she's choosing (just the coffee) rather than making her feel like she's declining something. Small copy, big difference in feel.
UX Principle
"A non-blocking upsell respects the user's primary intent. The order is already happening. This is just an offer — never a gate."

Confirmed — Nothing Left to Do

The order is in. Nadia knows exactly when it'll be ready, exactly where to pick it up, and her points have already updated. There's nothing left to tap. Just go get your coffee.

⏱️
ETA is the largest element on screen
"3 min" in 26px bold. It's the only number Nadia cares about at this point. Everything else is secondary information.
📍
"Mobile counter · Left side" — no ambiguity
Telling her exactly where to go in the store eliminates the awkward "where do I wait?" moment that often leads to joining the counter queue anyway.
📊
Progress bar animates — visual reassurance
A subtle animated bar shows the order moving. It solves the anxiety of "did it actually go through?" without requiring any action from Nadia.
⭐
Points updated in real time — 340 → 364
She can see the number changed. Not "points will be added soon." They're added now. This is the reward for using the app — delivered immediately.

No "rate your experience," no "share with friends," no promotions. The job is done. The best confirmation screen is one that asks nothing more of the user.
UX Principle
"The best confirmation screen has nothing to tap. The transaction is complete. Respect the user's time by letting them move on."
06 — What I Learned

The best UX is invisible.

Nadia won't notice the persistent login. She won't think about the fact that her order was pre-filled. She won't remark on the progress bar. She'll just think: this app is easy. That invisibility is the goal.

1
Removing friction is more valuable than adding features.
This redesign doesn't add a single new feature. It removes steps. Every eliminated tap was work the app was forcing Nadia to do that it should have been doing for her. The most impactful UX decisions were subtractions, not additions.
2
Research reveals the root cause, not just the symptoms.
Five different complaints in the reviews were actually one problem: the app doesn't respect the user's time. Once that root cause was clear, every design decision became obvious — because every decision was measured against one question: does this make Nadia's morning faster?
3
The upsell moment matters as much as the upsell itself.
The deal popup was originally designed to appear after the order was placed. Moving it to before checkout — after order review — changed it from an interruption to a service. Same feature, completely different user experience, just by changing its position in the flow.
4
Copy is a design decision.
"No thanks" vs "Just the coffee →". "Order confirmed" vs "Head over when you're ready, Nadia." Small copy changes that make the brand feel warm and human rather than transactional. Microcopy is where brand voice lives in a product.
